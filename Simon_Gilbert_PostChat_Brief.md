# Senior Enterprise Architect: my candid fit, gaps, and 30/60/90-day plan

Hi Simon,

Thanks for taking the time to chat. As promised, here is a candid summary of where I see my fit for the Senior Enterprise Architect role, where I am honest about gaps, and how I would approach the first 90 days.

## 1. What I clearly bring already

I believe I am a credible candidate for this role because my current UniSC work already overlaps with the core of the position:

- I am already operating in a university context and understand institutional priorities, stakeholder dynamics, governance sensitivities, and the pace of change inside UniSC.
- I have strong strategy-to-execution capability: operating model design, roadmap thinking, governance uplift, platform architecture, and executive-facing decision support.
- I have credible higher education architecture relevance through data governance, information risk, capability alignment, and enterprise platform direction.
- I can speak convincingly to Microsoft hybrid architecture, especially Azure Synapse, Purview, Power BI, cloud integration, governance, and assurance.
- I already work across business, information, applications, and technology concerns rather than only at a single technical layer.
- I have strong stakeholder engagement and translation ability across technical and non-technical audiences.
- I have experience with architecture-relevant frameworks and can apply them pragmatically rather than ceremonially.
- I have direct UniSC context, which is a material advantage over external candidates.

## 2. What is only partially covered today

These are the areas where I have adjacent evidence, but want to be upfront rather than overstate depth:

- Formal enterprise-wide business capability maturity assessment. I have capability alignment and governance uplift experience, but not yet a long track record running a formal university-wide capability maturity model.
- Business process modelling across multiple university entities. I have process mapping experience, but my background is stronger on governance, data, and platform architecture than BPM-led enterprise redesign.
- Application and technical portfolio rationalisation. I can credibly discuss simplification, standardisation, and reducing duplication, but I have not yet led a large formal application portfolio decommissioning program.
- Knowledge of student information systems, research management systems, and ERP platforms. I understand the higher education operating environment, but I have not had deep ownership of those specific platforms.
- Windows and Linux application architecture across both web and thick-client estates. I have enough technical depth to converse well, but this is not my strongest proven domain.
- Formal project management ownership across competing enterprise initiatives. I clearly coordinate delivery and priorities, but my profile is not framed as a classic enterprise program/project manager.
- Architecture assurance in formal IT governance forums. I have governance and assurance experience, but not yet a long explicit history of owning enterprise-wide EA gatekeeping processes.

## 3. What I see as real gaps

These are the areas I want to be candid about and turn into a ramp-up plan:

- LeanIX administration and platform ownership. I have not directly configured and maintained it.
- Preparing UniSC-specific Digital Initiative Briefs and using the internal impact-assessment process. I have not yet owned those artefacts.
- Deep institution-wide application portfolio visibility beyond my current data and analytics domain.

## 4. My candid positioning

I already bring the strategic architecture thinking, Microsoft hybrid platform depth, governance mindset, university context, and stakeholder credibility required for the role. The gaps are not about whether I can operate as an enterprise architect; they are about specific tools, internal processes, and a few parts of the broader application estate that I would close quickly in role.

I see myself as someone already doing enterprise architecture in substance, with a few specific enterprise-function tools and processes still to formalise at full institutional scale.

## 5. What I have and what I do not yet have

I already have the university context, strategic architecture mindset, governance experience, Microsoft hybrid architecture depth, and the ability to work across business and technical stakeholders. What I do not yet claim as a major strength is direct LeanIX platform ownership, formal end-to-end application portfolio rationalisation across the whole University, and some of the internal artefacts and processes such as DIBs if those sit specifically within this role. My view is that those are specific operational gaps, not fundamental capability gaps, and I would approach them with a disciplined ramp-up plan.

## 6. My 30/60/90-day plan

### First 30 days

Objective: establish enterprise context fast and remove ambiguity.

- Review UniSC strategy, divisional plans, current major initiatives, governance forums, architecture artefacts, and investment pathways. My goal in the first month is not to redesign anything prematurely, but to understand how the University currently frames priorities, how work gets funded, which initiatives are already committed, and where architecture can most credibly add value without creating drag.
- Learn the current enterprise architecture operating model: decision rights, governance cadence, assurance checkpoints, DIB workflow, and escalation paths. This means understanding who makes which decisions, what governance forums already exist, how architecture advice is currently consumed, where initiative assurance begins, and where the role is expected to influence rather than own outcomes directly.
- Assess the current LeanIX setup: meta-model, catalogue coverage, taxonomy, lifecycle fields, integrations, reporting, and data quality. My aim is to determine whether LeanIX is currently a trusted decision-support tool or mainly a partial repository, and to identify the minimum changes needed so it can support portfolio visibility, lifecycle management, and impact assessment quickly.
- Meet key stakeholders across IT, privacy, cybersecurity, applications, infrastructure, data, research, student systems, and business-facing portfolios. These conversations should establish where pain is highest, where trust already exists, where duplication or fragmentation is well known, and which leaders are likely to become early allies in shaping a more usable enterprise architecture practice.
- Build an initial enterprise map of the most material business capabilities, application domains, data dependencies, and platform risks. This does not need to be a perfect enterprise model in month one; it needs to be good enough to show where the University has critical dependencies, weak visibility, duplicated services, and high-risk integration points.
- Identify immediate pain points: duplicate services, unclear ownership, weak lifecycle visibility, poor integration transparency, and initiatives lacking architectural clarity. The value here is to show quickly that the architecture function can surface practical issues that matter to leaders and delivery teams, not just produce abstract models.
- Confirm what good looks like for the role in the first 6-12 months. I would want this explicitly agreed with you and other key leaders so the role is measured against institutional outcomes such as better portfolio visibility, clearer decision-making, stronger governance, and reduced duplication rather than vague expectations.

Deliverables by day 30:

- Current-state briefing covering strengths, risks, blind spots, and priority intervention areas. This would be a concise executive-level summary of what is working, where there are structural weaknesses, what cannot yet be seen clearly, and where early architecture intervention would have the highest institutional value.
- LeanIX health snapshot and uplift backlog. This would outline the present state of the platform, key data or taxonomy gaps, quick wins, and the staged improvements required to make it decision-useful rather than administratively burdensome.
- Stakeholder map and governance map. This would show who influences architecture outcomes, which forums matter, where approvals happen, where assurance is missing, and how the role should engage across technical and business leadership.
- Initial list of high-value portfolio rationalisation opportunities. This would not yet be a decommissioning program; it would be a prioritised view of where duplication, legacy risk, or unclear ownership appears substantial enough to investigate further.

### Days 31-60

Objective: turn discovery into a working enterprise architecture baseline.

- Stabilise and improve LeanIX so it becomes useful for decision-making rather than a passive repository. In this phase my focus would be on improving data completeness, cleaning up taxonomy where needed, clarifying ownership of records, and ensuring the platform can support real discussions about lifecycle, duplication, integration, and strategic fit.
- Define or refine capability mapping approach aligned to UniSC priorities. My intent is to create a capability view that is practical and decision-relevant, linking business priorities with applications, data, and technology dependencies rather than producing a theoretical model that teams do not use.
- Establish a practical architecture assurance process for initiatives, including impact assessment inputs and DIB support where needed. This should make it easier for projects and digital initiatives to get timely architecture input early enough to shape delivery, while setting a clear minimum bar for risk, integration, data, and platform considerations.
- Produce a first-cut view of application portfolio duplication, lifecycle risk, integration hotspots, and candidate consolidation areas. By this point there should be enough evidence to move beyond anecdote and identify where the University may be carrying avoidable cost, complexity, support burden, or risk because of overlapping or ageing services.
- Work with technical teams to map critical data and integration interdependencies across student, research, ERP, data, identity, and collaboration domains. This matters because many enterprise architecture failures come from hidden dependencies; making them visible early helps improve sequencing, avoid downstream rework, and support better investment decisions.
- Confirm architecture principles, decision criteria, and minimum documentation expectations. This creates consistency so stakeholders know how architecture recommendations will be made, what evidence is expected, and how initiatives will be assessed without introducing unnecessary overhead.

Deliverables by day 60:

- Agreed enterprise capability map v1. This should be good enough to support executive and planning conversations, showing the University's major capability areas and the key application, data, and technology relationships that matter most.
- Application and technology portfolio rationalisation shortlist. This would identify the most credible consolidation or remediation candidates, with a simple rationale such as duplication, high support cost, poor fit, lifecycle risk, or unnecessary complexity.
- Architecture assurance workflow with templates or guidance for initiative assessment. This would give delivery teams and governance bodies a repeatable way to bring initiatives through architecture review without ambiguity about what needs to be considered.
- LeanIX improvement plan in execution. By this point the plan should no longer be conceptual; ownership, priorities, data remediation tasks, and platform-use expectations should already be active.

### Days 61-90

Objective: move from baseline visibility to institutional traction.

- Publish a pragmatic enterprise architecture roadmap tied to strategic priorities, risk reduction, simplification, and platform sustainability. This roadmap should show what the University should sequence next, why those choices matter, what dependencies exist, and how architecture will support both transformation and operational resilience.
- Start targeted rationalisation work on the most obvious duplicate, legacy, or low-value services. My intent is to demonstrate that enterprise architecture is not only diagnostic but also action-oriented, beginning with a small number of credible opportunities where simplification can show visible benefit.
- Embed enterprise architecture into governance and investment decisions so initiatives are assessed earlier and more consistently. By this stage, architecture input should be becoming part of normal institutional decision-making rather than an optional late-stage review, improving both delivery quality and risk management.
- Support priority DIBs and impact assessments with clear architecture input. This is where the role begins to show practical value in real initiative flow, helping sponsors understand implications across capability, integration, data, platforms, security, and long-term maintainability.
- Identify capability maturity uplift actions for the next 6-12 months across business capability mapping, architecture assurance, portfolio management, and LeanIX data quality. This ensures the first 90 days are not treated as a standalone sprint, but as the foundation for a disciplined enterprise architecture maturity program.
- Set measurable indicators for EA value: reduced duplication, improved visibility, clearer ownership, better decision turnaround, and stronger investment confidence. If the role cannot show observable value, architecture will be seen as overhead, so establishing measures early is essential to long-term credibility.

Deliverables by day 90:

- Enterprise architecture roadmap v1. This should be an actionable document that leaders can use to guide sequencing, dependency management, and investment choices over the coming year rather than a generic future-state diagram.
- Priority portfolio rationalisation recommendations. These would present a small number of evidence-based recommendations with clear rationale, likely benefits, major dependencies, and areas requiring further validation.
- Operating cadence for architecture assurance and governance. This would define how often architecture reviews occur, where decisions are made, what gets escalated, and how the architecture function remains engaged with live initiatives.
- 6-12 month maturity uplift plan for EA practice, LeanIX, and portfolio visibility. This would set out how the function moves from initial stabilisation to a more mature enterprise architecture capability with stronger data quality, better coverage, and clearer institutional value.
